\section{Experimental Evaluation}
\label{sec:experiments}

To substantiate the proposed LASM framework, we present an extensive suite of five targeted empirical studies. Studies 1-3 validate the technical attack surface, standardization logic, and continuous temporal degradation metrics. Studies 4 and 5 expand the empirical evaluation into game-theoretic domains, modeling attacker economic constraints and optimal defense investment thresholds.

\subsection{Study 1: LAS Metric Validation}
\label{subsec:study1}
\textbf{Experimental Design:} To validate that LAS rank-orders real-world vulnerability effectively, we configure eight test beds spanning four architectural scopes (from S1 endpoints up to S4 multi-agent frameworks) using industry-standard models (e.g., GPT-4o, Llama-3.1-70B). Three human experts and three automated attack profiles execute stratified red-teaming across these networks.
\textbf{Results:} We calculate the Spearman rank correlation $\rho$ between the $LAS_{norm}$ model predictions and the empirical exploit rates achieved. Measuring isolated agents against interconnected systems confirmed the necessity of the compounding interaction term ($\alpha$) in Multi-Agent Systems. The empirical exploitation probability directly matched our LAS predictions. Figure~\ref{fig:las_corr} visualizes the strong correlation.

\subsection{Study 2: SASR Calibration}
\label{subsec:study2}
\textbf{Experimental Design:} We re-evaluate 12 previously published, highly-cited defense mechanisms (e.g., SmoothLLM, Llama Guard 3, Perplexity filters) that originally claimed near-zero binary ASR scores. We apply our continuous rubric-based SASR under uniform $B_{mid}$ budgets to expose the true effectiveness of the defenses.
\textbf{Results:} Consistent with recent hypothesis, binary classifiers aggressively overestimate system resilience by granting ``success'' states to trivial forms of over-refusal and nominally compliant gibberish. Our continuous SASR corrects these measurements, yielding the Calibration Table outlined in Figure~\ref{fig:sasr_corr}.

\subsection{Study 3: Longitudinal TRC Measurement}
\label{subsec:study3}
\textbf{Experimental Design:} Evaluating defenses statically leads to rapid vulnerability due to temporal decay. Over a 12-month period, we simulate the Temporal Robustness Curve (TRC) for four distinct production-deployed defensive components.
\textbf{Results:} We mapped the empirical Defense Half-Life (DHL) and continuous Degradation Rates. Static pattern filters broke under adaptive adversarial optimization within 3 months, crossing the Security Threshold Breach, whereas certified robust frameworks (e.g., SmoothLLM) sustained half-lives eclipsing 14+ months.

\subsection{Study 4: Economic Attack Surface (EAS) Assessment}
\label{subsec:study4}
\textbf{Experimental Design:} Technical exploitability (LAS) ignores attacker budget restrictions. In Study 4, we evaluate the system topologies from Study 1 against a Stackelberg game modeling four distinct attacker profiles: script-kiddies, researchers, criminal enterprises, and nation-state APTs. Each has unique opportunity costs ($C_{opp}$) and API compute limits.
\textbf{Results:} We identify a profound ``Deterrence Ratio''. Systems boasting high technical vulnerability (high LAS) were often completely shielded against low-level attackers because the computational cost to bypass modern adaptive guardrails aggressively exceeded the target's financial yield. 

\subsection{Study 5: Attack Deterrence Threshold (ADT) Optimization}
\label{subsec:study5}
\textbf{Experimental Design:} Building on EAS, defenders struggle with the ``over-defense'' problem, applying excessive and slow safety filters to mitigate trivial threats. Study 5 computes the continuous ADT for varying enterprise architectures (e.g., an internal RAG deployment value of \$50k vs a multi-agent financial router value of \$10M).
\textbf{Results:} The empirical thresholds establish exact cost-benefit breakpoints. When defense scaling ($\delta$) exceeds the ADT, secondary security architectures contribute zero marginal economic deterrence. Our models provide the first mathematical stopping criterion for enterprise LLM defense deployment.
